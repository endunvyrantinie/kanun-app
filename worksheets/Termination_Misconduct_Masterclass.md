# Termination & Misconduct Masterclass: Navigating Malaysian Employment Law

## Overview

This masterclass focuses on the critical aspects of employee termination and misconduct under the Employment Act 1955 (EA 1955) and the Industrial Relations Act 1967 (IRA 1967) in Malaysia. Understanding these provisions is crucial for both employers and employees to ensure fair practice and legal compliance. Each question is followed by a detailed explanation, citing relevant sections of the Acts, to provide a comprehensive understanding.

## Instructions

Answer each question to the best of your knowledge. Review the explanations to grasp the legal nuances surrounding termination and misconduct.

### Section 1: Termination Procedures and Notice

**Question 1 (MCQ):** Where the contract is silent, an employee with 3 years of service is entitled to notice of termination of:

A. 4 weeks
B. 6 weeks
C. 8 weeks
D. 12 weeks

**Answer:** B. 6 weeks

**Explanation:** Section 12(2) of the EA 1955 specifies notice periods: 4 weeks (<2 yrs), 6 weeks (2 to <5 yrs), 8 weeks (5+ yrs), where the contract is silent.

---

**Question 2 (True/False):** Before terminating an employee for misconduct, the employer must hold a 'due inquiry' — a domestic inquiry where the employee can answer the allegations.

A. True
B. False

**Answer:** A. True

**Explanation:** Section 14(1) of the EA 1955 requires a due inquiry before dismissal for misconduct. Skipping it is a common reason dismissals are overturned at the Industrial Court.

---

**Question 3 (MCQ):** An employee dismissed without just cause and excuse may file a representation under the Industrial Relations Act 1967 within:

A. 14 days
B. 30 days
C. 60 days
D. 90 days

**Answer:** C. 60 days

**Explanation:** Section 20 of the IRA 1967 states that a representation must be made to the Director General of Industrial Relations within 60 days of dismissal.

---

**Question 4 (MCQ):** Termination benefits under the Employment (Termination and Lay-Off Benefits) Regulations 1980 apply primarily to employees earning:

A. Any salary level
B. Up to RM2,000/month
C. Up to RM4,000/month
D. Up to RM5,000/month

**Answer:** C. Up to RM4,000/month

**Explanation:** Although EA 1955 itself was extended to all employees in 2023, the Employment (Termination and Lay-Off Benefits) Regulations 1980 remain limited to employees earning RM4,000 and below.

### Section 2: Misconduct and Compliance

**Question 5 (MCQ):** A manager sets up a side business that competes with the employer using the employer's contacts. Discovered after termination on unrelated grounds. Best legal route for the employer:

A. No remedy — he's already terminated
B. Civil claim for breach of fiduciary duty / confidentiality / contract (post-employment restraints, where enforceable) and unjust enrichment, plus possible criminal liability for criminal breach of trust
C. Industrial Court claim
D. Report only to MyEG

**Answer:** B. Civil claim for breach of fiduciary duty / confidentiality / contract (post-employment restraints, where enforceable) and unjust enrichment, plus possible criminal liability for criminal breach of trust

**Explanation:** Post-termination breaches of fiduciary duty and use of confidential information are pursued in civil courts. Criminal Breach of Trust (CBT) under the Penal Code may apply to dishonest misappropriation. Restraint clauses must be reasonable to be enforceable under Section 28 Contracts Act 1950.

**Question 6 (Decision):** Your finance lead asks: 'Can we treat the new hires as interns for the first 6 months to avoid EPF and SOCSO contributions?'

A. Yes — call them interns to save cost
B. No — if the role is a real job, statutory contributions apply regardless of title
C. Yes for the first 3 months only
D. Ask them to sign a waiver

**Answer:** B. No — if the role is a real job, statutory contributions apply regardless of title

**Explanation:** Substance over form. If the role isn't a genuine internship aligned with study, the 'intern' label is a sham. EPF/SOCSO obligations attach by the nature of the working relationship. Misclassification exposes the company to back-contributions, penalties, and possible offence proceedings.

**Question 7 (Decision):** A reference check on an outgoing employee returns: 'Was she a good employee? Did she steal anything? Why did she leave?'

A. Share everything you know to be helpful
B. Confirm dates of employment and role only; flag separately if asked under specific lawful process
C. Refuse to answer at all
D. Make up something neutral to be safe

**Answer:** B. Confirm dates of employment and role only; flag separately if asked under specific lawful process

**Explanation:** Standard best practice. Limits PDPA and defamation risk while remaining truthful. Factual confirmation of employment dates is generally low-risk. Sharing everything is risky due to defamation exposure and PDPA concerns. Fabricating information creates exposure for the employer.
